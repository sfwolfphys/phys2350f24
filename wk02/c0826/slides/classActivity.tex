    \hypertarget{eulers-method-for-determining-position-and-velocity-given-acceleration}{%
      \section{Euler's method for determining position and velocity given
        acceleration}\label{eulers-method-for-determining-position-and-velocity-given-acceleration}}

    Given numerical values for: - An initial time: \(t_0\) (usually, we pick 0 sec) - An
    initial position: \(x_0\) (Depends on the problem, will \emph{always} be given) - An
    initial velocity: \(v_0\) (Depends on the problem, will \emph{always} be given) - A known
    acceleration function \(a(t)\) (Depends on the problem, will be given for now)

    In general, our goal is to make a table full of numbers that allows you to fill in the
    table below:

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}llll@{}}
  \toprule t & x & v & a\tabularnewline \midrule \endhead \(t_0\) & \(x_0\) & \(v_0\) &

Usually, you are looking for a certain final time, or are given a condition when you should

In general, you calculate the next element based off of the previous elements
\begin{align*}
  t_{j+1} &= t_j + \delta t \\
  x_{j+1} &= x_j + v_j \delta t \\
  v_{j+1} &= v_j + a_j \delta t \\
  a_{j+1} &= a(t_{j+1}) \\
\end{align*}

This lets you determine the next row of your table based on the previous row of the table.

    \hypertarget{appendix-the-analytic-method-for-solving-this-problem}{%
      \subsection{Appendix: The analytic method for solving this
        problem:}\label{appendix-the-analytic-method-for-solving-this-problem}}

\[
  a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = e^{-t} \quad v(0)=0 \quad x(0)=0
\] \#\#\# Step 1: Integrate the acceleration to get the velocity

\begin{align*}
  v(t) &= \int a(t) dt \\
       &= \int e^{-t} dt \\
       &= -e^{-t} + C_1
\end{align*}

where \(C_1\) is an arbitrary constant that is determined by the\ldots{}

\hypertarget{step-2-apply-the-condition-vt00}{%
  \subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Step 2: Apply the condition \(v(t=0)=0\)}{Step 2: Apply the
      condition v(t=0)=0}}\label{step-2-apply-the-condition-vt00}}

\[
  0 = -e^0 + C_1 \implies C_1=1
\]
Therefore
\[
  v(t) = 1-e^{-t}
\]

\hypertarget{step-3-integrate-the-velocity-to-find-the-position}{%
  \subsubsection{Step 3: Integrate the velocity to find the
    position}\label{step-3-integrate-the-velocity-to-find-the-position}}


\begin{align*}
  x(t) &= \int v(t) dt \\
       &= \int \left(1-e^{-t}\right) dt \\
       &= t + e^{-t} + C_2
\end{align*}
where \(C_2\) is an arbitrary constant that is determined by the\ldots{}

\hypertarget{step-4-apply-the-condition-xt00}{%
  \subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Step 4: Apply the condition \(x(t=0)=0\)}{Step 4: Apply the
      condition x(t=0)=0}}\label{step-4-apply-the-condition-xt00}}

\[
  0 = 0 + e^0 + C_2 \implies C_2 = -1
\] Therefore \[ x(t) = t - e^{-t} - 1
\]

These functions for \(x(t)\) and \(v(t)\) are the red lines that I have added to my graphs as
the ``actual'' answer.

\hypertarget{differential-equations-lingo}{%
  \subsection{Differential Equations lingo}\label{differential-equations-lingo}}

I don't want you to focus on learning this for this class, but I am attempting to put what you
are learning here in a context that will be useful for future coursework so that when it comes
up again, it hopefully looks/feels a little familiar.

The equation \[ a(t) = e^{-t}
\] can be framed in one of two ways.

\begin{enumerate}
  \def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}  \tightlist
  \item A 2nd order differential equation (second order because it has a 2nd derivative in
  it): \[ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = e^{-t}
  \]
  \item Two, 1st order differential equations:
  \[ \frac{dv}{dt} = e^{-t} \quad \frac{dx}{dt} = v(t)
  \]
\end{enumerate}

Either way, the system has a \emph{generalized solution} (that is, one where we don't have any
initial values or \emph{boundary conditions}).  If we want to have a \emph{specific solution},
we need to know the value of the functions \(x(t)\) and \(v(t)\) for some time value. (It need
not be zero, like we had in this example).
